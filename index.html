Food Recall Enforcement

Food recalls are safety actions used to remove contaminated or incorrectly labeled products from the market. This story looks at why recalls happen, how serious they are, and how long they remain active.

1. Background

There is a rigorous process of classifying danger, notifying the public, and removing products from store shelves. This includes cases of mislabeling products, and violations of laws enforced by the Food and Drug Administration (FDA). The process helps ensure that unsafe foods are removed in a timely manner, as removing these products from the market via food recalls is more efficient than going through juridical processes. Despite these attempts to improve efficiency, food recall processes only officially end once the FDA has verified that all contaminated items are accounted for, a logistical cycle that can often take over a year to complete. Hence, we explored the causes for food recall to demonstrate a need for a better process for protecting public health.

2. Leading Causes of FDA Food Recalls

Key Point

The two largest causes of food recalls are listeria and undeclared allergens. These are both highly preventable but also highly dangerous to affected individuals. According to the FDA, listeria tends to thrive in unsanitary food production, and when it contaminates food, can lead to risk of consumers suffering consequences ranging from nausea and a fever to life-threatening consequences.

3. Recalls by Year and Severity

Key Point

A majority of the food recalls are under Class I (44.2%) and 2 (50.0%), while only 5.8% of food recalls are categorized as Class 3. Given that Class 1 includes food recalls that are the most severe while Class 3 includes food recalls that are the least severe, this suggests that most food recalls are likely to have adverse health consequences and may lead to death.

4. How Long Food Recalls Remain Active

Key Point

Food recalls are not resolved immediately. Many cases take up over 100 days to resolve and can reach up to over 300 days , regardless of the severity of the recall. This is a dangerous amount of time for products to still be shelved despite FDA awareness of its risks, calling for better procedures that address these risks more efficiently.

5. Takeaway

Food recalls are caused mainly by issues that can be easily preventable, but most are classified as cases of high severity. These recalls can also take a long time to get resolved, which might pose a risk for people still consuming the products. Hence, the FDA should work towards a more efficient solution that reduces risk of unsafe food consumption at a more timely manner.
